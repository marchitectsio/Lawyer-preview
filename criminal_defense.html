Process

Three step timeline used on most cases

Every case has unique facts, but the sequence is familiar. We use it to act early and build leverage for dismissal, reduction, or the best possible resolution.

Step 1

The arrest

Stay calm and stay silent. Do not try to explain the situation to police. Ask for a lawyer. Document names, locations, and any witnesses the moment you can do so safely.

Step 2

The arraignment

This is the first court appearance. Bail and protective orders are often decided here. We appear with you, ask for reasonable release conditions, and set the case up for strong defense work.

Step 3

Pre trial hearings

We challenge the evidence. That includes suppression motions, credibility issues, and technical flaws in testing or police procedure. Many cases are won here long before a trial date.

Defense strategy

What your defense can attack

The prosecution has to prove every element beyond a reasonable doubt. We look for the breaks in the chain.

  • Legality of the stop and search including warrants, consent, and detention length
  • Reliability of witnesses including bias, motive, and contradictions
  • Forensics and testing including chain of custody and methodology
  • Intent because many crimes require a specific mental state
  • Alternative explanations that fit the evidence better than the accusation

Frequently asked questions

Should I talk to police to clear it up?

In most cases, no. People unintentionally fill gaps for the prosecution. Politely request counsel and let your attorney handle communication.

How fast should I hire a lawyer?

As soon as possible. The earliest decisions often control bail, protective orders, evidence, and negotiation posture.

Can a case be dismissed early?

Yes. Dismissals can happen when evidence is excluded, witnesses do not cooperate, or the prosecution cannot prove required elements. Early motion practice helps.
